# Asymmetric rising tide
*Economic uplift without intentional affordability mechanisms displaces the very people a Christoindustrialist set out to serve.*

> The narrator's most aching line in the Bentonville documentary is the gentle one: "Many boats have been lifted with the rising tide, but not all of them." The land barons of the original town are now millionaires. Their grandchildren's classmates cannot afford a starter home in their own hometown. A Christofuturist who builds a thriving village and lets the asymmetric tide run unopposed has not served the poor. They have priced the poor out of the room where the lift was happening.

---

## What the asymmetry looks like

In Benton County, the median household income is roughly $94K, about 54% above the Arkansas state median of $61K, and the poverty rate is around 8%, roughly half of the state's 15%. By most macro measures, Bentonville is winning. The community looks healthy.

Zoom in and the picture is more complicated. The average home value is $488K. The documentary tours a $2M+ mansion two minutes from the town square. The fifth-generation farmer the documentary interviews tells the camera, plainly, that the cost of living is going up and he does not know how young people are going to afford housing. He is also the man who became wealthy from rising land values around his farm. He is both the beneficiary and the witness of the asymmetry.

The Reddit thread the documentary references names the same pattern in different words: the town's charm is being diluted and the cost of living is climbing fast.

## Why the tide is asymmetric by default

Economic uplift in an anchored village runs through three channels, and only one of them lifts everyone. A Christoindustrialist should understand all three before claiming the lift will reach the people they set out to serve:

- **Wages.** Direct employment at the anchor industry. Best for talent the industry actively wants. Worst for service workers, retirees, and anyone whose role does not scale with the anchor's growth.
- **Asset prices.** Land, housing, and local businesses appreciate as demand crowds in. Best for incumbents who already owned property at low values. Worst for renters, young families forming households, and incoming low-income workers.
- **Public goods.** Parks, trails, museums, schools, libraries funded by anchor philanthropy. Available to everyone in proximity, in theory. In practice, the people most able to enjoy them are the ones who can afford to live close enough to use them.

The asset-price channel is the one that does the displacement. Without an offsetting affordability mechanism, the people who arrive after the lift starts get to enjoy the public goods only if they can pay the property tax on a $488K home or pay the rent that capitalizes that home value.

This pattern compounds into what FaithWalk OS names [the mortgage-snare](https://faithwalk.garysheng.com/concepts/the-mortgage-snare): an entire generation of would-be village families locked out of formation-stable housing in the exact places they were called to.

## What a Christoindustrialist must engineer instead

The corrective is not to slow the lift. It is to build affordability mechanisms into the lift from the start, before the asymmetry becomes a fact on the ground. Concrete moves a Christoindustrialist should plan for in year one rather than year fifteen:

- **Land trusts and church-owned housing.** Take significant residential capacity off the speculative market early. A community land trust or church-held housing co-op can sell homes at the cost of construction plus a small inflation index, keeping them affordable across generations.
- **Income-indexed housing allocations.** Reserve a fraction of new housing for service workers, single parents, retirees, and the original residents. Tie the price to local median income for the role, not to market comparables.
- **Industry-side wage floors.** The anchor Christoindustry pays at the top of the regional band for every role, not only the elite roles. The aim is that someone who works full-time at the village's anchor can comfortably live in the village.
- **Original-resident protections.** The people who were there before the lift get explicit affordability priority. The 5th-generation farmer in Bentonville did fine; his classmates did not. A Christofuturist village owes the people of the original place a non-token claim on the lift.
- **Visible public mechanisms.** The affordability moves are documented, governed by elders, and observable to the village. Secrecy invites cynicism. Transparency invites accountability.

## The Christofuturist test

The simplest test a Christoindustrialist can run on their village every year is the question: who could not afford to live here at year ten who lived here at year one? If the answer is "everyone who is not already wealthy," the lift has become a displacement. If the answer is "no one was forced out, and the original residents are visibly thriving in the new village," the lift has been engineered.

Bentonville did not run that test. A Christofuturist village must.
