# Christocompound
*The physical convergence point inside a Christofuturist village where worship, food, learning, daycare, healthcare, coworking, and community meet. Often a single multipurpose building or campus.*

> A Christofuturist village needs a place where everyday life and worship occupy the same physical envelope. Where the daycare and the sanctuary share a wall. Where the coworking floor is upstairs from the food hall and downstairs from the chapel. Where the gym, the school, and the medical clinic are walkable to the same congregation. The Christocompound is that envelope. It is the architectural answer to the disintegration of modern American life.

---

## The architectural pattern

A Christocompound is one of two shapes:

- **One multipurpose building.** A single structure that holds worship at its gravitational center and surrounds it with daycare, school, food, coworking, gym, clinic, and gathering space. Compact, vertical, dense.
- **A clustered campus.** Several buildings on contiguous land, walkable end-to-end, organized around the sanctuary as the visual and spiritual center. Less compact, more institutional, capable of greater scale.

Worship sits at the center in both shapes. The other uses radiate outward in proportion to how often a household needs them. Daycare and food are closest. Specialized clinics and gym facilities are slightly farther. The point is that a resident can do a full day of life on foot, with worship as the geographic and temporal anchor.

## The prototype

The Crossover in Cedar Park, Texas, is the closest existing prototype of the single-building Christocompound. Whitestone Church anchors the campus and shares the envelope with food, learning, and wellness uses that operate on weekday rhythms. The pattern is documented in the Refound America manifesto as the architectural blueprint a Christoindustrialist should study.

A Christoindustrialist visiting the Crossover is looking at the small-scale, single-building expression of what a mature Christofuturist village will need at multi-building campus scale.

## Tension with sacred-center-first urbanism

The Christocompound and [sacred-center-first urbanism](./sacred-center-first-urbanism) are related and distinct.

The Christocompound is the everything-under-one-roof or one-campus pattern. The sacred core is contained inside the same architectural envelope as the everyday uses. Walkability is built into the building or the cluster.

Sacred-center-first urbanism is the broader town-scale pattern. The sacred core is at the literal center of the town's street grid, with surrounding parcels organized around it. The town itself is walkable to the sacred core, but the daycare and the gym and the food hall might live in separate buildings on separate parcels.

[Ave Maria, Florida](../case-studies/ave-maria-florida) embodies the sacred-center-first pattern at town scale. The parish church is at the visual center of the master plan, and the university, school, and town center cluster around it across several parcels. The Crossover embodies the Christocompound pattern at building scale. Both are valid. Village stage and population scale determine which fits. Small early-stage villages start with a single Christocompound. Mature villages add sacred-center-first urbanism at the surrounding town scale.

## Capital and operational requirements

Scale matters. A starter Christocompound might be a 20,000-square-foot multipurpose building with worship space, daycare, a kitchen, a small clinic, and shared workspace, capitalized in the low-to-mid eight figures. A mature Christocompound campus might be ten acres, multiple buildings, a parish church, a school, a clinic, a coworking facility, and a gathering hall, capitalized at nine figures or beyond.

Most Christoindustrialists will start at the lower end. The pattern is the same at both scales. The capital model is the same: founding-stack patron capital plus retained earnings from the Christoindustry. See [private-capital-replaces-public-infrastructure](./private-capital-replaces-public-infrastructure) for how that funding model actually works.

## What the Christocompound does for the village

- **Gravitational pull.** Residents naturally orbit the compound. The day's rhythms (school drop-off, lunch, midday worship, evening gathering) all converge in one place.
- **Inter-household fellowship.** Neighbors meet over food and shared programming, not just at Sunday services. The compound is where weak ties become strong ones.
- **Formation rhythms.** Children's daycare and adult worship in the same envelope reinforces the worldview as the default, not as a Sunday add-on. Catechesis becomes ambient.
- **Operational efficiency.** Shared HVAC, shared kitchens, shared parking, shared facilities staff. The per-household cost of community is lower than maintaining five separate institutional buildings.

## Failure modes

- **Building too big too early.** A Christoindustrialist who funds a 100,000-square-foot facility before there are 300 households to fill it has built a beautiful, half-empty cathedral. Phase the build with the population.
- **Building too institutional too fast.** Compounds that look like office parks rather than homes lose the family feel. The architecture has to invite, not intimidate.
- **Building without the surrounding population to fill it.** A Christocompound disconnected from a residential base becomes a regional event venue. It is the residential proximity (households within ten minutes' walk) that makes it a Christocompound rather than a megachurch.
