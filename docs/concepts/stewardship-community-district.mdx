# Stewardship community district
*Florida's special-purpose legal-infrastructure vehicle. A public-law chassis for roads, utilities, assessments, bonds, and conservation that lets a founder + developer plan a town without waiting for an incorporated municipality. Ave Maria's institutional backbone.*

> A Christoindustrialist trying to build a village on raw land in the 2020s does not have time to walk the multi-decade path through municipal incorporation. The state has already invented the right tool for the job. The stewardship community district is what Florida hands to a founder with the conviction and capital to plant a town. Other states have similar vehicles under different names. Find yours before you pour foundations.

---

## What it is

A stewardship community district is a special-purpose governmental district created under Florida statute. It is the legal-infrastructure chassis underneath a master-planned town. It is allowed to:

- Issue bonds for public infrastructure.
- Assess property to pay back those bonds.
- Operate water, wastewater, stormwater, and reclaimed-water systems.
- Build and maintain roads, sidewalks, and recreation facilities.
- Plan and govern schools, healthcare facilities, transportation, security, and waste collection.
- Hold and manage conservation set-asides.

In other words, it is a public-law toolkit for everything an incorporated municipality would do, scoped to the boundaries of the planned community, and available on the founder-developer's timeline rather than the county's.

The Ave Maria Stewardship Community District was created in 2004. By 2007 the first resident moved in. That is the speed advantage at scale.

## Why it matters for Christoindustrialist village-builders

Public process is designed for legitimacy at the scale of an existing population. A greenfield Christoindustrialist village does not yet have a population to be legitimate to. Trying to use ordinary municipal incorporation as the legal chassis means waiting for a population to arrive before you can build the infrastructure that would attract the population. The stewardship district inverts that order. The legal vehicle exists before the residents do. The infrastructure can be built ahead of the demand curve.

This is the same speed-versus-legitimacy tradeoff that [private capital replaces public infrastructure](./private-capital-replaces-public-infrastructure) names at the funding layer. The stewardship district is the legal twin of that pattern. Together they let a founder-developer compress decades of municipal process into months.

## The pattern

The replicable institutional move has two layers paired together:

1. **The legal-infrastructure vehicle.** The stewardship community district itself. Public-law powers, bond authority, and statutory governance.
2. **The land-use framework.** A stewardship-receiving-area zoning plan, or its equivalent, that sets permitted uses, building standards, setbacks, road design, and architectural rules. This is what governs the form of the town.

One vehicle finances and governs infrastructure. The other governs form and development rights. A Christoindustrialist should expect to set up both before breaking ground.

## Tradeoffs

The honest costs a Christoindustrialist must name on day one:

- **Landowner-controlled governance.** Stewardship district boards historically originate in landowner elections on a one-acre, one-vote basis, not normal one-person, one-vote municipal democracy. Resident control transitions slowly. Ave Maria's third resident-elected seat will not arrive until more than half the district property is platted for development. That is a long window of resident disenfranchisement.
- **Assessments fall on residents long-term.** The bond debt is paid by property assessments. Residents inherit the obligation when they buy in. The founder gets the speed; the residents pay the long-tail bill.
- **Dependency on the founder-developer entity.** Until the resident transition completes, the district is steered by the founder, the family land partner, or the development entity. That is the same structural fragility that [single-anchor dependency risk](./single-anchor-dependency-risk) names elsewhere.

These tradeoffs are manageable but not avoidable. A Christofuturist village design should document the transition plan from day one rather than improvising under stress.

## Other states with similar vehicles

Florida is unusual in the breadth of its stewardship-district statute, but the underlying pattern exists in many states under different names. A Christoindustrialist scoping a greenfield site should look for:

- **Community development districts** (Florida and a handful of other states).
- **Public improvement districts** (Texas).
- **Municipal improvement districts** (various Western states).
- **Tax increment financing districts** (most US states; less governance authority but similar capital tooling).
- **Special service areas** (Illinois and others).

The specific statute varies. The pattern is the same: a special-purpose legal vehicle that gives a founder-developer team public-finance powers without waiting for incorporated municipality status. The Christoindustrialist's first land-use lawyer's first job is to find the right one in the host state.
