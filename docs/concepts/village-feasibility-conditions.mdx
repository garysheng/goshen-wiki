# Village feasibility conditions
*Four conditions a Christoindustrialist needs to honestly assess before claiming a village strategy is feasible.*

> The Morning Brew documentary closes on the narrator's argument that Bentonville is unrepeatable. Hidden inside that claim is a four-condition framework. A Christoindustrialist who reads it as a verdict will quit before they start. A Christoindustrialist who reads it as a checklist, with the understanding that Spirit-led obedience substitutes for whichever conditions are missing, gets to walk into the village strategy with their eyes open.

---

## The four conditions

The narrator names them, intentionally or not, in the closing voiceover. Walmart was founded "at just the right moment in American capitalism, grew into one of the most valuable companies in the world, the largest retailer on the planet and never left, in a low-cost, low-regulation state, with the founding family still in control." Parse that sentence and four conditions fall out.

### 1. A dominant or dominant-trajectory anchor industry

The anchor industry has to be large enough, or on a credible path to being large enough, to fund a town. Walmart is the largest retailer on the planet. A Christoindustry does not need to be that large in absolute terms; it needs to be dominant enough to provide stable employment, stable revenue, and stable capital reinvestment across the village's first generation.

Self-assessment questions:

- Is the industry's total addressable market sufficient to support a village of one to ten thousand families?
- Is the industry's competitive position defensible across at least one generational cycle?
- If the industry plateaus at half of its current trajectory, does the village still work?

### 2. Founding-family commitment across multiple generations

Bentonville works because the Walton family is still in control after three generations and shows no sign of leaving. Most company-town attempts in American history failed when the founding family lost interest, sold the company, or moved away. Multi-generational commitment is what turns a town into a place.

A Christoindustrialist's version of this condition is not necessarily one biological family. It can be a covenant of founding families, a church plant, or a religious order whose continuity is institutional rather than genetic. The point is durable, sticky, multi-generational stewardship.

Self-assessment questions:

- Have the founding leaders made an explicit, public, covenant-grade commitment to the place?
- Is there a documented succession plan that survives the founder's death or departure?
- Are the next-generation leaders being formed inside the village, not outside it?

### 3. A low-friction host location

Arkansas's low-cost, low-regulation, low-permitting environment is a real part of why Bentonville could be built fast. A Christoindustrialist trying to plant a village in a high-friction state will pay an order-of-magnitude tax in time, money, and political energy.

Friction includes: zoning regimes, environmental review timelines, permitting backlogs, labor regulations, tax regimes, hostile local politics, hostile state politics, and the cost of land.

Self-assessment questions:

- Where, geographically, does the village's planned scope clear permitting in months rather than years?
- Are the local and state political environments at least neutral, ideally friendly, to the village's mission?
- Is the cost of land at a price point that allows for the affordability mechanisms described in [asymmetric rising tide](./asymmetric-rising-tide)?

### 4. Sufficient continuous time

Bentonville's population tripled in the last 25 years, and the foundation for that growth was laid across the preceding 50. A Christofuturist village strategy that is planned on a five-year horizon is planning to fail. The plan needs a 30-year horizon with intermediate checkpoints.

Self-assessment questions:

- Has the founder genuinely committed the next 20 to 30 years of their life to this place?
- Is the capital structure patient enough to wait two decades for the cluster effect to fully form?
- Are the leadership development pipelines designed for slow apprenticeship, not fast role-filling?

## How to use the framework

A Christoindustrialist runs the four conditions against their own situation. Each one will land in one of three states:

- **Real.** The condition is fully present. Move forward, document the strength, plan to preserve it.
- **Partial.** The condition is present in some dimensions and missing in others. Identify the gap, design a substitute, monitor it.
- **Missing.** The condition is genuinely absent. Decide whether the substitute is durable, or whether the village strategy is actually not the right move for this situation.

The Christofuturist supplement that the narrator does not name is the fifth condition: Christ as the actual anchor. A village strategy where the four secular conditions are weak but the Christ-centered foundation is strong can still work, because the Spirit closes gaps that capital cannot. A village strategy where the four secular conditions are perfect but Christ is decorative will produce another Bentonville, with the same fragilities the case study documents.

## Why Bentonville is "unrepeatable" and why a Christoindustrialist should not believe that claim

The narrator's irreproducibility argument is, technically, an argument about a specific combination of conditions arising again at the exact scale Walmart did. That combination is rare. The framework itself is not. Many smaller versions of the four conditions cluster regularly. The Christofuturist village does not need to be Bentonville-scale to be Kingdom-impactful.

What a Christoindustrialist actually needs to internalize is that the four conditions are individually rare and collectively rarer, which is why the village strategy is a real, hard, multi-decade undertaking. Not impossible. Hard. The right response to hard is preparation, not abandonment.
