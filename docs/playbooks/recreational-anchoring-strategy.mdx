# Recreational anchoring strategy
*Pick a distinctive recreational identity, invest at a world-class level, and attract the demographic that values it.*

> A Christoindustrialist who treats culture as something that emerges organically from the people who happen to show up will get the culture that happens to show up. A Christoindustrialist who treats recreational identity as a designed input to the village's gravity gets to choose which kind of family the village pulls in. Bentonville chose mountain bikers, on purpose, and the choice shaped the next twenty years of the town's character. The Christofuturist version of this move is the same shape with Christ-aligned substance.

---

## When to run this

You are the lead Christoindustrialist for a village in years one through ten, the anchor industry exists or is forming, and the village's cultural identity is still genuinely up for grabs. If you are inheriting a thirty-year-old town with a fixed identity, this is the wrong playbook; you are in cultural-stewardship territory, not cultural-engineering territory.

## Prerequisites

- A founding leader or family with personal credibility in the recreational identity you want to seed (Tom and Steuart Walton are avid mountain bikers; they were not faking it).
- Capital sufficient to invest at a world-class level in the chosen identity, on the order of tens of millions of dollars across a decade.
- Geographic features that allow the chosen identity to be expressed authentically (Bentonville sits on Ozark terrain that is genuinely good for biking; a flat plains town would have been the wrong host for that play).
- A theological green light: the recreational identity must be congruent with the village's Christ-centered formation, not orthogonal to it.

## Steps

1. **Choose the recreational identity deliberately.** Pick something distinctive, defensible, family-friendly, and consistent with the village's spiritual formation. The chosen identity should attract the demographic the Christoindustry needs and should not contradict the Christocompound's discipleship culture. Candidates include: trail running, sacred-music festivals, classical-education tournaments, equestrian disciplines, sailing, sacred dance, climbing, rowing, observational astronomy. The wrong choices for a Christofuturist village include identities that pull in adjacent vice cultures, gambling, party tourism, or aggressively secular subcultures.
2. **Invest at the top of the band, not the middle.** Mid-tier trails create a mid-tier town. Bentonville's identity unlocked because the spend was world-class. Plan for ten years of sustained investment that produces facilities a destination-traveler would book a flight to use.
3. **Recruit the credibility anchors.** Bentonville did this by hosting the US national mountain biking team's HQ in the world's first bikable building. The Christofuturist equivalents are denomination-recognized festivals, training programs that produce nationally known graduates, or sanctioned tournaments that draw the elite tier of the chosen identity.
4. **Brand the village around the identity at a specific moment.** Bentonville did this in 2020, calling itself the mountain biking capital of the world. Pick a date, declare the identity publicly, anchor it to the visible infrastructure, and let the story compound. A Christoindustrialist might pair this with a public liturgy: a dedication service, a covenant renewal, a community-wide commissioning.
5. **Sequence supporting infrastructure.** Schools that teach the discipline. Local shops, gear makers, instructors. Lodging for visiting families. Trails or facilities that scale with the demographic. Each layer reinforces the identity and increases the village's lock-in for the demographic that values it.
6. **Pair the identity with the village's evangelism strategy.** The recreational identity is also a doorway. Visiting families come for the trails, the festival, the tournament; they encounter the Christocompound, the worship, the discipleship, and the village's full life. The recreational layer becomes the front porch of the village's witness.

## What good looks like at the end

- Outsiders use the recreational identity as the village's primary description before they say anything about the anchor industry.
- The demographic the Christoindustry needs for retention is over-represented in the village's age 25 to 45 cohort, because they came for the recreation and stayed for the calling.
- A meaningful fraction of recreational visitors have, over the years, transitioned from visitors to families who moved in. The front porch is converting.
- The recreational layer is self-sustaining, with a network of local instructors, businesses, and community leaders carrying it forward without the founder's continuous attention.

## Common failure modes

- **Identity inauthenticity** → if the founders do not actually love the chosen recreation, the village smells like a marketing exercise within five years → fix: choose the identity from the founders' real loves first, not from market analysis first.
- **Spiritually orthogonal identity** → the recreation pulls in a demographic whose values undermine the Christocompound's formation → fix: pre-test the chosen identity against the village's catechism and discipleship culture before declaring it, and walk away if there is a real conflict.
- **Under-investment** → mid-tier facilities attract mid-tier interest, identity never anchors → fix: commit to the world-class budget upfront or pick a smaller identity that can be world-class at your capital level.
- **Identity that does not survive geography** → the chosen recreation is mismatched to the host place → fix: pick an identity the land naturally supports, the way the Ozarks support biking.
- **Identity becomes the whole point** → the village forgets its anchor industry and its discipleship mission and becomes a recreational town with a Christian veneer → fix: keep the recreational layer subordinate to the Christoindustry and the discipleship culture, and audit yearly.
